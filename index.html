Phase 1 Course Goals


1. Foster the creative habits of mind

From just a few projects, I think I've portrayed some breadth in curiosity regarding what creation can be. My Project Fuego is a little straight-forward, mixing in a few flourishes to dip my toe in the water. My New Media Webtext expands outward and delves into more thoughtful and pointedly intentioned use of media—it asks the viewer to seriously consider the developments in climate change and how we consider climate change. Then, my Branching Webtext turns around and dabbles in the more fun, fictional, and imaginative applications of web design. Although, it draws on very familiar and personal material (studying in Hillman), and I feel as though using a concept rooted so mundanely into my routine helps me practice finding the story and creativity in my everyday experiences.

2. Engage with others in a workshop environment.

Without experiencing others' creations in workshop, I would not have come up with this concept for my New Media Webtext Experiment. While I have always been interested in media analysis, having so many peers mention their interests in using digital media to explore natural sciences made the idea stick in my mind. It made me curious about the intersections of those two interests; how have we historically used media to discuss the natural world, and how is that reflected in the modern day? Also, in terms of contributing to workshop, I would like to imagine that my technical experience with GitHub has been helpful to a few of my peers as we work through the crafting process.

3. Think deeply about craft.

The difference in the amount of customization in my Project Fuego and my New Media Webtext Experiment seems to show an improvement in my comfort in HTML and CSS. I made several changes to the styling of my New Media Webtext Experiment to try and enhance the experience. For example, the text color is off-black to emulate how an old newspaper wears and bleaches over time. It is a small and easy change, yet one carefully considered for that webtext's purposes. Furthermore, I have experience with Twine as a tool to create narratives, and had a goal in mind to mimic what I have previously gotten out of Twine through my own Branching Webtext. Although structurally simple, I believe that my resulting website satisfies that goal. Overall, I feel comfortable that my HTML & CSS abilities are capable enough to execute what I want out of a webtext.

4. Engage in sustained research and skills development.

I drew on the ideas I generated as I read "The Language of New Media Art" to finalize my concept for my New Media Wetext Experiment. From there, I searched online and found tutorials from W3Schools to achieve the two-column newspaper effect. I also took time to look into archival newspapers to recreate and establish my theme, and read through the New York Times to find something that called back to that theme. The project did not originate as a project on climate change, but through that exploration (and the inspiration from my peers) my webtext cultivated as such.

5. Learn to present your work.

I am generally very uncomfortable with presenting myself and my work, but I would say I have been putting quite a bit of effort into volunteering myself and making my voice heard in this class during workshops. Although not explicitly asked for, I commented out all the HTML for the next few modules in this portfolio so that this website's only focus is on the work that already exists. Lastly, although very different projects, I did my best to find thumbnails of my different projects that would look somewhat cohesive, and if I had to ascribe a central theme to all my work in this phase, they all center around the intentionality and the processes behind communication.
